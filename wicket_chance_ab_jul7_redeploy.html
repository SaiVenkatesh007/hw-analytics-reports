Key findings

Narrative summary

Mean wickets up +22–32%: Redeployed Exp=2 delivers a large mechanical lift — e.g. 0–50k 2.59→3.37 (+30%). Effect holds batting first (+22–33%) and bowling first (+21–32%); not a chase-only artefact.
Scores fall 3–8%; innings length flat: Mean scores −7.8% (0–50k) to −3.4% (100k+). Balls faced ~26 in both arms; RPB down 2–8%. WR dips modestly (0–50k 48.5→43.3, −5.2pp) — not the fake bot read.
Mild shot adaptation: Zeros/ones up ~+1pp; fours/sixes down ~−1pp; twos flat. Fours wicket rates up +5–7pp. Strongest below 75k — risk reallocation, not full behaviour change.
First 2 overs: +12–20% early wickets: Mechanical lift visible before adaptation. Runs in first 2 overs down −1 to −4%.
vs Jun clean read: Earlier +9–13% wicket bump with flat scores was under-powered. Jul 7 redeploy at Exp=2 moves outcomes meaningfully while keeping innings length stable.

Shot selection

Did players change their shots?

Shown in percentage points (test share − base share). Shot shares sum to 100%, so pp is the honest comparison.

Shot selection insights

Almost no behavioural change: Across every bracket and view, the shot mix shifts less than 1pp. Players faced a higher wicket risk but kept playing essentially the same shots.
Fours are the only consistent mover: Down −0.4 to −0.9pp, with a small offsetting rise in ones (+0.4 to +0.5pp). A slight pull-back from the riskiest scoring shot toward safe singles — but tiny.
First 2 overs are virtually identical: Players open the same way regardless of arm. The minor fours→ones drift only appears later in the innings, and mostly at higher brackets.
Implication: The wicket-chance increase was too small to trigger the intended squad-diversification behaviour. A larger increase would be needed to change how players actually play.
