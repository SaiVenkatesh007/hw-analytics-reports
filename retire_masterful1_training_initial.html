Key findings

What exposure shows

No batting roster expansion after Retire unlock: unique batsmen mean 3.2 → 3.1, median flat at 3. If Retire helped users invest in finishers via a wider batting set, we would expect unique batsmen up — that signal is absent.
Unique players overall flat-to-down: mean 6.7 → 6.0, median stays 6. Breadth does not increase post-exposure.
Intensity rises for the typical user: overall median level-ups 71 → 80; batsman median 40 → 44. Users train harder on a similar roster — depth, not expansion.
Mild tilt toward batsman levels: batsman share of level-ups 50.5% → 52.8%. Directionally consistent with batting focus, but still on ~3 batsmen.
Cohort is highly engaged: 100% train in ±30d; even Q1 median is 76 level-ups across 6 players. The Retire question is about allocation, not whether they train.

Recommendation

One call

Do not read exposure-only Before/After as evidence that Retire expands batting investment. Next priority: tag actual Retire usage and compare Retire users vs non-users on unique batsmen trained (and batsman level-ups) before vs after Masterful 1.

Product lens

Usage gap: Until we know who actually retired a player after Masterful 1, we cannot attribute Before/After training shifts to the feature vs general post-rank-up grind.
